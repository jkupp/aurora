Part 1

Our Restless Sun

The Sun constantly streams charged particles into space — the solar wind. But some days it erupts. A coronal mass ejection (CME) hurls a billion-ton cloud of magnetized plasma toward Earth. That's when the sky comes alive. And we're near solar maximum right now.

Part 2

Earth's
Invisible Armor

The magnetosphere deflects almost all the solar wind — like a vast invisible shield. But at the poles, magnetic field lines converge and funnel inward. Those are the entry points. Think of it like a neon sign: electricity through gas makes colored light. Same physics.

The Aurora Doesn't Fall Randomly — It Follows an Oval

The aurora follows a ring called the auroral oval, centered on Earth's magnetic pole — not the geographic North Pole. Tromsø, Reykjavík, and Fairbanks all sit inside or near this ring. Montana and Scotland sit well below it — which matters a great deal for forecasting.

Part 3

Why These
Colors?

The aurora is a live readout of Earth's layered atmosphere. Oxygen at 100 km glows green. Oxygen higher up glows red. Nitrogen traces the lower edges in blue and purple. Every color you see has an address in the sky.

The Rhythm

Why is the aurora so hard to predict? Because three completely different timescales govern it — and most people only know one. Days. Hours. Minutes. And a spike on a magnetometer means one thing above all else: "The door is opening."

Where You Are
Changes Everything
